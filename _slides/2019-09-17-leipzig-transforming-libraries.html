### Some final reflections * Creating self-reporting data initially takes more time to create, but it scales better in the long term. * Do we have the libraries and scholars have the patience to create this data? * Do we have the kinds pressures and incentives pushing people to toward long term results over short term gains?
### Some final reflections * Are libraries really interested in using (at some level) data provided by third party scholars or research groups/archives? * Research groups often work at a level of micro-granularity, while libraries and museums often work at macro level. * This creates a kind of one way street. Scholars use the macro data and the develop micro data. * How can we build systems and releationship where this micro data can be re-incorporated/re-connected with library macro-data. * The above examples illustrate the possibility, but libraries can't deal with idosyncratic standards and protocols from individual research groups. * How can we create usable standards across research domains that nevertheless allow the creation and preservation and micro, domain specific data?
### Some final reflections * Are we ready to create new kinds of data archives, that include, for example entities for every line in every manuscript?
### Some final reflections * A lot of coordination between institutions and scholarly groups is required to create this kind of interoperability. * How can scholars and/or scholarly research communities be a part of the conversation about standards and ontology creation?
